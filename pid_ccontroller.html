Proportional–Integral–Derivative Controller (PID Controller)

A PID controller is a feedback-based control loop mechanism used for continuous control and automatic adjustment of machines and processes.

It compares the target value (Setpoint, SP) with the actual system value (Process Variable, PV), and computes the error e(t) = SP - PV.

Corrective Actions

  • Proportional (P)
  • Integral (I)
  • Derivative (D)

The PID controller applies a combination of these terms for accurate and responsive control.

Control Function:
Output = Kp × e(t) + Ki × ∫e(t)dt + Kd × de(t)/dt

Where:
Ki = Kp / Ti
Kd = Kp × Td

Proportional Term

The proportional term generates output based on the current error, scaled by Kp. A higher gain yields faster response but can cause instability.

Steady-State Error (SSE)

SSE is the residual error in the system. It's proportional to process gain and inversely proportional to Kp. Adding an integral term or bias helps reduce SSE.

Integral Term

The integral term accumulates past errors and applies correction accordingly. It helps eliminate steady-state error:

Ki × ∫e(t) dt

But too much integral gain may lead to overshooting the target value.

Derivative Term

This term anticipates future error by evaluating the rate of change:

Kd × de(t)/dt

It improves system stability and settling time but is sensitive to noise. Often filtered or avoided in noisy environments.
