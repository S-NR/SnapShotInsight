3.3 Proportional–Integral–Derivative Controller (PID Controller)

The PID controller is a feedback-based control loop mechanism commonly used to manage machines and processes that require continuous control and automatic adjustment.

The controller compares the desired value (Setpoint, SP) with the actual system value (Process Variable, PV), and the difference e(t) = SP - PV drives the correction.

Corrective Actions

  • Proportional (P)
  • Integral (I)
  • Derivative (D)

The key strength of a PID controller lies in combining the influence of all three components — P, I, and D — to optimize control performance.

Formula:
Output = Kp * e(t) + Ki * ∫e(t)dt + Kd * de(t)/dt
where:
Ki = Kp / Ti
Kd = Kp * Td

3.3.1 Proportional Term

The proportional term outputs a value directly proportional to the current error.

If the error is large, the response is large (based on gain Kp). However, proportional control alone always leaves a steady-state error.

Steady-State Error (SSE)

The SSE is the remaining difference between SP and PV. It occurs because the proportional controller requires a non-zero error to maintain output. SSE is:

  • Proportional to process gain
  • Inversely proportional to Kp

It can be reduced by adding a bias or using an integral term.

3.3.2 Integral Term

The integral term accumulates past error values over time and compensates for residual steady-state errors.

The contribution is calculated as:

Ki * ∫e(t) dt

It boosts control when the error persists. However, excessive use may lead to overshooting due to accumulated effects from previous errors.

3.3.3 Derivative Term

This term predicts future error trends by looking at how quickly the error is changing:

Kd * de(t)/dt

It acts like a damping component, improving system stability and response time. However, it's sensitive to noise and may require filtering.

Because of its complexity and real-world sensitivity, derivative control is less frequently used (estimated in about 25% of cases).
